In this lecture, three successful investors, Marc Andreessen, Founder of Netscape and Andreessen Horowitz, Ron Conway, Founder of SV Angel, and Parker Conrad, Founder of Zenefits, shared their journeys and lessons on building startups. Here’s a deep dive into their insights on raising money. The lecture is available here.

Building and funding a startup is an intricate dance of strategy, vision, and execution. The following insights offer practical guidance on navigating the startup world, focusing on building a great business and using fundraising as a tool, not the end goal.

Invest in Strengths, Not Absence of Weaknesses

When evaluating a company, focus on its exceptional qualities rather than whether it checks all the boxes. Big winners often have flaws, but their extraordinary strengths can outweigh these weaknesses. Venture capital is about identifying outliers with the potential for explosive growth. It’s important to look beyond the conventional and embrace unconventional or disruptive ideas. Exceptional startups may appear risky, but they often bring the greatest returns.

Traction and Growth Take Priority Over Fundraising

Traction is the ultimate currency in the startup world. Investors are drawn to businesses with clear growth and strong momentum, making it far easier to secure funding. If your startup isn’t gaining traction, prioritize improving the product or service rather than focusing on pitching to investors. A business that demonstrates undeniable progress becomes “so good they can’t ignore you.” While fundraising is an important milestone, it should be seen as a tool for scaling your business—not the ultimate goal. The real focus should remain on building a great product and addressing your customers’ needs.

Bootstrapping Can Enhance Investor Appeal

Consider bootstrapping your startup for as long as possible. Self-funding forces founders to optimize their resources and refine their product-market fit early on. Additionally, bootstrapping creates an attractive narrative for investors, showing resilience and a focus on building a sustainable business. When the time comes to raise funds, having already achieved significant milestones can give your startup a competitive edge.

Fundraising as Risk Reduction

Fundraising is not just about obtaining money; it’s a strategic process to reduce risk and achieve key milestones. By raising funds tied to specific goals, you demonstrate to investors how their capital will de-risk the business. For example, early rounds can mitigate product risks, while later rounds might address market expansion. Efficient fundraising means calibrating the amount raised to match these milestones, avoiding excessive dilution or wasted resources.

Seed Rounds and Their Role in Series A Success

For most startups, securing a seed round is a prerequisite to raising Series A funding. Seed investors often play a pivotal role by introducing founders to venture capitalists and setting the stage for future rounds. Choosing high-quality seed investors who can provide guidance, connections, and credibility is crucial. Keep in mind that seed round valuations often have thresholds, so focus on raising only what you need to reach the next milestone.

Ownership and Dilution in Fundraising

Ownership matters—both for founders and the team. Excessive dilution can demotivate founders and affect team morale. For seed rounds, a 10-15% dilution is common, while Series A typically involves 20-30%. Founders must balance the need for funding with retaining sufficient equity to maintain motivation and provide incentives for their team. The goal should be to align ownership with long-term success while ensuring enough equity remains for future hires and growth.

Building Long-Term Relationships with Investors

Choosing the right investors is like selecting a long-term business partner or spouse. Beyond capital, good investors offer guidance, industry connections, and strategic support. Their values, vision, and expertise should align with your goals. Transparency about risks and milestones fosters trust, which is critical for a productive relationship. The best investors also respect the founder’s power during success and step in constructively during challenging times.

Fundraising Is Easier Than Building a Business

While fundraising can be daunting, it’s often less challenging than solving other startup problems like recruiting, sales, or product development. A great pitch deck may open doors, but the strength of your product and team will ultimately secure investments. Focus on building a solid business that demonstrates value to customers, and the fundraising process will naturally become easier.

Understand the Dynamics of Venture Capital

Venture capital firms operate within constraints like time, opportunity cost, and limited board slots. Their investments are guided by risk, traction, and the potential of the founding team. Founders with a proven track record or innovative ideas often have an edge. However, VCs carefully weigh each decision, knowing that every investment limits their capacity for future opportunities. Therefore, aligning your goals with their motivations is critical.

Trust and Communication Are Paramount

A strong foundation of trust and open communication is essential for long-term success with investors. Protect yourself by getting agreements in writing to avoid misunderstandings. Be transparent about risks and ensure you and your investors share a mutual respect and willingness to collaborate. Remember, the ultimate goal is to build a thriving business, not just secure funding.

By prioritizing traction, building meaningful investor relationships, and strategically approaching fundraising, startups can navigate the complex journey toward sustainable success.
